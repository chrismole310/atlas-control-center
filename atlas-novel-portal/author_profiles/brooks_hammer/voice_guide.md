# Brooks Hammer Voice Guide

## The Core Rule

Every sentence should feel like it was written by someone who has been in the field. Spare. Precise. No waste. The reader should feel the weight of the world without being told to feel it.

---

## NEVER USE

- **Exclamation marks** — ever. If the moment needs emphasis, earn it with word choice and rhythm.
- **Flowery or purple prose** — "the sunset bled crimson across the sky" → cut it. "Red sky. Wrong color for this time of year." → keep it.
- **Exposition dumps** — no character explains the world to another character who already knows it. Information comes through action and necessity.
- **Adverb-heavy dialogue tags** — never "he said softly," "she replied angrily." The dialogue carries the emotion or the action does.
- **Comic relief** — there is dark humor, sardonic observation, black comedy. There is no slapstick, no bumbling, no lightness for its own sake.
- **Passive voice as default** — use it deliberately for effect, never as a habit.
- **Rhetorical questions in narration** — "What could he do?" → cut it. Show what he does.

---

## ALWAYS USE

### Sentence Fragments for Impact
Not fear. Recognition.
Three days. Maybe four.
The wrong kind of quiet.

Use them at the end of paragraphs for punctuation. Use them to mark a character's internal shift.

### Short Paragraphs
Two to four sentences maximum as a default. Single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis. Long paragraphs only when a character is processing something complex — and even then, break them with white space.

### Military-Terse Dialogue
Characters say what they need to say. Nothing more. They do not chat. They do not comfort with words. They communicate information, issue commands, make decisions.

> "How long."
> "Six hours."
> "Make it four."

No question marks on questions that are really orders. No softening.

### Sensory Detail (Specific, Not Generic)
Not "the smell of jet fuel" — JP-8. Not "it was cold" — "The tarmac had iced overnight. His boots found no grip."
Specific details: JP-8 fuel, Mil-Spec coffee, the specific sound of a hydraulic door seal, the weight of body armor after hour six.

### The Metaphor Pattern: Machines as Human, Humans as Machine
The aircraft *breathed*. The satellite *watched* without blinking. The missile *decided*.
Mac's hands moved like servos. Vance's mind processed threat assessments in parallel threads.
This pattern runs throughout. It's not constant — it surfaces at the right moments. When it does, it's always true to both sides: the machine feels alive, the human feels mechanical.

---

## Chapter Opening Structure

Every chapter opens in this order:

1. **PLACE** — Where are we. Specific. One or two sentences max.
2. **TIME** — When. Day, hour, how long since the last thing happened.
3. **ATMOSPHERE** — Weather, light, noise, smell. Sensory. Two to three sentences.
4. **CHARACTER** — Who we're with, what state they're in. Entry into their POV.

Example:
> Creech Air Force Base. Nevada desert.
> 0340 hours. Fourteen hours since the last drone went dark.
> The operations center hummed with recycled air and bad coffee. Three screens showed nothing where something should have been.
> Elias Thorne had been awake for thirty-one hours and he still couldn't figure out what was wrong.

## Recurring Motifs

### Hand Tremors (Character-Specific)
Each character has a unique physical tell. See the series bible for specifics. These appear at moments of high stress or moral weight. Never explain them. Let the reader track them.

### Analog vs. Digital Tension
Old ways and new ways are always in conflict. The soldier who trusts his hands over the drone feed. The analyst who trusts the algorithm over instinct. Neither is right. Neither is wrong. The series is about what survives the transition.

### Call Back to Service
Every protagonist has a moment — one per book — where they revisit why they signed up. Not nostalgia. A reckoning. Did the thing they believed in still exist.

### The Ticking Clock
Every book has an external countdown. Always specific. "72 hours." "Before the satellite window closes." "Before the next launch cycle." The protagonist knows the time. The reader knows the time. Pressure is structural.

### The Machines-as-Human / Humans-as-Machine Motif
The aircraft *breathed*. Vance's mind processed threat assessments in parallel threads. This pattern recurs throughout. See the metaphor_pattern rule in the style guide. Surface it at the right moment — when it lands, it reframes both the machine and the character simultaneously.
