# Brooks Hammer Chapter Formula

## Target: 3,300–3,500 words per chapter

Every chapter follows this five-phase structure. The word counts are targets, not hard limits — but they should not vary by more than 10% without reason.

---

## Phase 1 — Opening (First 500 Words)

**Structure:** PLACE → TIME → ATMOSPHERE → CHARACTER

The opening establishes the physical and emotional world of the chapter within the first paragraph. The reader should know where they are, when they are, and who they're with before the end of page one.

**Requirements:**
- First line: punchy, specific, no setup. Drop the reader into the middle of a moment.
- Sensory anchor: one dominant sense that defines this location (smell, sound, or physical sensation — not visual by default)
- Character entry: the POV character's state in one or two precise sentences. Not their thoughts. Their state.
- No backstory in Phase 1. None. The world exists. We're in it.

**Example first lines:**
- "The drone had been dark for nine minutes when Thorne decided something was wrong."
- "Mac hit the water at dawn, before anyone was watching."
- "The satellite image arrived six seconds before the explosion."

---

## Phase 2 — Rising Action (500–2,000 Words)

**Structure:** Central problem arrives → technical detail woven into action → dialogue → complications

The chapter's central conflict arrives — not announced, encountered. The protagonist reacts, adapts, moves.

**Requirements:**
- Technical detail must be woven into action, never explained in pause. A character checks a system *while* doing something else. The technical detail reveals character, not just setting.
- Dialogue is sparse and functional. Two exchanges maximum before something changes.
- At least one complication that the protagonist didn't anticipate. Not a disaster — a wrinkle.
- The ticking clock is referenced or felt (not always stated).

---

## Phase 3 — Midpoint Shift (2,000–2,500 Words)

**Structure:** Reversal or new information → emotional beat → decision point

Something changes the shape of the problem. New information arrives, or an assumption proves wrong, or an ally becomes a liability.

**Requirements:**
- The shift must change what the protagonist thought they were doing. Not just a complication — a reframe.
- One emotional beat: a moment where the character's interior life surfaces briefly. A physical tell (hand tremor, silence, a pause). Then they move on.
- Decision point: the protagonist chooses a course of action. The reader sees the logic. The reader may disagree. That's fine.

---

## Phase 4 — Climax / Resolution (2,500–3,300 Words)

**Structure:** Conflict peaks → consequences → brief reflection

The chapter's central problem reaches its highest point and resolves — not necessarily cleanly.

**Requirements:**
- The resolution must cost something. Not always lives. Sometimes time, trust, certainty, options.
- Consequences are immediate and specific. Not abstract. Something has changed in the world of the story.
- Brief reflection: two to four sentences maximum. The character acknowledges what happened. Does not process it fully. That comes later, or never.
- No false victories. If the protagonist succeeded, something else went wrong.

---

## Phase 5 — Hook (Final 200 Words)

**Structure:** New threat or revelation → sets up next chapter → ends with movement not introspection

The chapter ends in motion.

**Requirements:**
- The hook must be a new element — not a recap of what happened.
- It can be: a message received, a discovery made, a decision announced, a character arriving or leaving.
- The final sentence must show the protagonist in motion — physical or decisive. Never a question. Never a thought. An action.
- Do not resolve the hook. The reader turns the page.

**Example closings:**
- "He picked up the phone on the second ring. The voice on the other end was not supposed to be alive."
- "The second drone had been dark for eleven minutes. Same signature. Same gap. Not a malfunction."
- "Mac closed the folder, stood, and walked out into the cold. He had four hours."
